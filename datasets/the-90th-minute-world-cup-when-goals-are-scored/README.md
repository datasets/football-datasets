# The 90th-Minute World Cup

Stoppage time used to be where matches went to die. Now it's where they come alive.

We timed all **2,720 goals** in men's World Cup history — the exact minute each one hit
the net — and one trend cuts straight through the noise: **the World Cup goal keeps
arriving later.** The late winner isn't a fluke of modern memory. It's in the data.

## One in twelve

In the fully-timed era — **1990 to 2022** — **114 goals arrived in stoppage time. That's
8.2% of every goal scored, better than one in twelve.** Whole games now turn after the
fourth official lifts the board.

Nowhere more than **Russia 2018, when 13.0% of all goals came after the whistle should
have blown** — the highest share any World Cup has ever recorded.

## The clock keeps drifting

A World Cup goal used to land, on average, just before the hour mark — around the **48th
minute** through the mid-century tournaments. By **Qatar 2022 that average had drifted to
the 55.7th minute.** Defenses got better, benches got deeper, and the decisive moment kept
sliding toward the end.

- **2022** — goals averaging the **55.7th minute**, **12.2%** of them in stoppage.
- **2018** — **13.0%** in stoppage, the record share.
- **1998** — the first edition where stoppage goals cracked double digits: **11.1%**.

*(One quirk for the pedants: the single latest-scoring edition on record is actually
**Italy 1990, at 59.3** — a famously goal-starved tournament where what little scoring
there was came late.)*

## One honest line about the chart

The source only started tagging the **added-time minute in 1990.** Every earlier goal is
filed as regulation time. That doesn't mean nobody scored in stoppage before 1990 — of
course they did — it means we can't *see* which goals those were. So the stoppage-time
story runs from **1990 onward**, and we don't pretend otherwise. Goal minute and goals
per match, on the other hand, are solid all the way back to 1930.

## The game didn't slow down — it spread out

Scoring peaked long ago: **Switzerland 1954, a ludicrous 5.38 goals a game.** Modern World
Cups sit in a tight band of **2.5 to 2.7**, with Qatar 2022 at **2.69**. But the goals are
distributed differently now — fewer first-half routs, more drama saved for the death.

The 90th minute stopped being an ending. It became the plot.
